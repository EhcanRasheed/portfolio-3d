The Other Side of My Stack

How It Started

When I started my degree, I wasn't planning to become a full-stack developer. I wanted to be a cybersecurity specialist. For the first two and a half years of my graduation, that was the plan and I committed to it completely. I studied every cyber-related subject harder than anything else, went beyond the curriculum with self-study, and that grind is what eventually earned me an internship at a national-level institute in Pakistan where I worked in Threat Intelligence, learning firsthand how real threats are detected, analyzed, and dealt with at a national scale.

But somewhere in that journey, my curiosity toward AI and full-stack development kept growing louder. So I made a call, pivoted completely, and poured everything into that instead. It became my sole passion and it is what I do now. That said, two and a half years of serious work does not just disappear. Cybersecurity is still something I follow as a hobby and if you asked me to switch back tomorrow, it would not take long. That chapter shaped how I think about systems permanently.

What I Actually Know

This is not surface-level familiarity. I spent years building genuine depth across the full spectrum of cybersecurity concepts.

Networking and Protocols

My foundation starts at the network layer. I have strong command over the OSI Model and TCP/IP Stack, subnetting and CIDR notation, and how traffic actually moves through systems. I understand DNS Security through DNSSEC, DHCP Snooping, ARP Spoofing, and the full mechanics of HTTP/S alongside SSL/TLS handshakes. I have used Wireshark for packet sniffing and traffic analysis, which taught me more about how networks behave under pressure than any textbook could.

Cryptography and Identity

I understand both symmetric and asymmetric encryption, hashing algorithms including SHA-256 and MD5, cryptographic salting and peppering, and the full structure of Public Key Infrastructure. On the identity side I am well versed in Identity and Access Management, Multi-Factor Authentication, the Principle of Least Privilege, and the mechanics of OAuth 2.0 and API Security.

Threats, Attacks and Defenses

I know how attacks are structured and how they are stopped. I have studied the Cyber Kill Chain and the MITRE ATT&CK Framework in depth. My knowledge of offensive techniques covers SQL Injection, Cross-Site Scripting, Cross-Site Request Forgery, Man-in-the-Middle attacks, Buffer Overflow vulnerabilities, Social Engineering including phishing and pretexting, DoS and DDoS mitigation strategies, and the basics of Reverse Engineering and Malware Analysis both static and dynamic.

On the defensive side I understand firewalls across stateless, stateful, and next-generation implementations, Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems, Zero Trust Architecture, Network Segmentation and DMZs, VPNs through both IPsec and SSL, SIEM platforms, Endpoint Detection and Response, and Data Loss Prevention.

Secure Development

Security in software is something I think about from the design stage, not the deployment stage. I have strong familiarity with the OWASP Top 10, Secure Software Development Lifecycle principles, Linux hardening and permissions, Windows Registry and Event Logs, Active Directory Security and Kerberos, and using Python for security automation. I also understand Container Security across Docker and Kubernetes environments, Cloud Security through the Shared Responsibility Model, Wireless Security standards including WPA2 and WPA3, and the basics of IoT Security and Firmware Analysis.

Governance and Compliance

I have studied the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, ISO/IEC 27001 standards, Governance Risk and Compliance principles, Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery planning, Incident Response phases, and Digital Forensics including Chain of Custody procedures. Vulnerability Assessment versus Penetration Testing is a distinction I understand both conceptually and practically.

Real Field Experience

Internship

Completed a Cybersecurity Threat Intelligence internship at a national institute in Pakistan, where I gained hands-on experience in how threats are detected, analyzed, and dealt with at a national level. Working in a real threat intelligence environment rather than a simulated one is an experience that very few people at my stage of career have had, and it remains one of the most formative things I have done.

Security Projects

Real-time Keylogger Detector

A Windows security tool built to detect and neutralize unauthorized keystroke logging in real time. When a keylogger process is identified, the tool alerts the user and attempts to terminate the threat before any data is captured or exfiltrated.

Spam-Rok

An intelligent spam message detector designed to identify and filter malicious and unwanted communications. Built to handle the specific linguistic patterns of the Pakistani digital context including Urdu, Roman Urdu, and English, which most generic spam filters fail to catch reliably.

File Integrity Checker

A utility that uses cryptographic hashing to establish file baselines and monitor for any unauthorized changes. Any deviation from the stored hash triggers an alert, catching tampering or corruption that would otherwise go undetected silently.

Why It Still Matters

Cybersecurity is not what I sell. But it is part of what makes me a more careful, more aware, and frankly more dangerous builder than someone who never went down that road. When I architect a SaaS platform or build an AI system, that two and a half years is already in the room with me. It is why I think about how a system breaks before I think about how it ships.
